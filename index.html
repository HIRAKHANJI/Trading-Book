Candlestick Patterns & Price Action

Candlestick charting was introduced to the Western world by Steve Nison, CMT, and is now the standard visualization used by institutional and retail traders globally. His work, originally drawing on Japanese rice trading techniques dating back centuries, forms the basis of modern price action reading.

Daily 45-min split

20 min study · 20 min chart pattern hunting on BTC/USDT 4H · 5 min journal

Candlestick Anatomy

  • Body: Distance between open and close. Green/white = bullish (close > open). Red/black = bearish (close < open).
  • Wicks/Shadows: Show the high and low. Long lower wick = buyers pushed price back up. Long upper wick = sellers pushed price back down.

Timeframes

TimeframeUseSignal quality
1m / 5mScalpingLow — mostly noise
15m / 1HDay trading entriesMedium
4HYour primary entry TFHigh
DailyBias / directionHighest
WeeklyMacro trendContext only

Key Patterns to Learn

Single Candle

  • Hammer: Small body, long lower wick. At support = potential reversal up.
  • Shooting Star: Small body, long upper wick. At resistance = potential reversal down.
  • Doji: Open ≈ close. Indecision. Context determines direction.
  • Engulfing: Current candle completely engulfs previous. Strong signal at key levels.

Multi-Candle

  • Morning/Evening Star: Three-candle reversal pattern
  • Three White Soldiers / Black Crows: Three consecutive strong candles in one direction

THE critical concept from Nison: Candlestick patterns are CONTEXT-DEPENDENT. A hammer at a key support level with volume confirmation means something. A hammer floating in the middle of nowhere means nothing. Context is everything.

Chart Patterns

Chart patterns are formations created by price movement that signal potential continuation or reversal. They work because thousands of traders see the same pattern and act on it, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy — but only when confirmed by volume.

Reversal Patterns

Head & Shoulders (and Inverse)

Structure: Three peaks — left shoulder, head (highest), right shoulder. The "neckline" connects the lows between them. A break below the neckline with volume confirms the reversal.

Target: Measure the distance from head to neckline. Project that distance down from the neckline break point.

Inverse H&S: Same pattern flipped — appears at bottoms, signals bullish reversal.

Double Top / Double Bottom

Double Top (M shape): Price hits resistance twice, fails both times. Break below the middle trough confirms. Bearish reversal.

Double Bottom (W shape): Price hits support twice, holds both times. Break above the middle peak confirms. Bullish reversal.

Volume should decrease on the second touch and increase on the breakout.

Continuation Patterns

Triangles

TypeShapeBias
AscendingFlat top, rising bottomUsually bullish — buyers getting more aggressive
DescendingFlat bottom, falling topUsually bearish — sellers getting more aggressive
SymmetricalConverging linesNeutral — breaks in direction of prior trend

Flags & Pennants

Bull Flag: Sharp move up (flagpole), then tight downward channel (flag). Breakout continues upward. Bear Flag: Mirror image.

Pennant: Like a flag but the consolidation forms a small symmetrical triangle instead of a channel.

Wedges

Rising Wedge: Both trendlines slope up but converge. Usually bearish — momentum is dying. Falling Wedge: Both slope down but converge. Usually bullish.

The golden rule of chart patterns: A pattern is NOT confirmed until it BREAKS. Drawing a head and shoulders doesn't mean it will play out. Wait for the neckline break with volume. Anticipation without confirmation is how pattern traders lose money.

Support, Resistance & Market Structure

Daily 45-min split

20 min study · 20 min draw S/R levels on BTC/ETH daily charts · 5 min journal

Support & Resistance

Support = price level where buying pressure historically prevents further decline. Resistance = where selling pressure prevents further advance. These form because of order clusters — many traders placed orders at similar levels.

Key principle: S/R levels are ZONES, not exact lines. The market doesn't respect your precise horizontal line. Draw areas, not numbers.

How to Draw Levels Properly

  • Use candle bodies primarily (not just wicks)
  • Look for multiple touches — the more times price respects a level, the stronger it is
  • Draw zones with some width, not single lines
  • Higher timeframe levels carry more weight than lower timeframe levels

Market Structure

StructurePatternBias
BullishHigher Highs (HH) + Higher Lows (HL)Look for longs
BearishLower Highs (LH) + Lower Lows (LL)Look for shorts
RangingPrice bouncing between S/RTrade the range boundaries

Key Structure Concepts

  • Break of Structure (BOS): Price breaks a key swing point, confirming trend continuation
  • Change of Character (CHoCH): Price breaks structure in the OPPOSITE direction — potential reversal signal

Why this matters for leverage: S/R levels are where you place stop losses and take profits. Wrong levels with leverage = liquidation. Right levels = controlled risk.

Fibonacci Retracements & Extensions

Fibonacci levels are derived from the mathematical sequence discovered by Leonardo of Pisa. In trading, these ratios mark levels where price tends to find support or resistance during pullbacks and projections. They work because enough market participants watch them — creating order clusters at these levels.

Key Retracement Levels

LevelMeaningStrength
0.236 (23.6%)Shallow pullback — very strong trendWeak
0.382 (38.2%)First significant support in a pullbackMedium
0.5 (50%)Halfway point — "equilibrium" (not a true Fib number but widely watched)Strong
0.618 (61.8%)The "golden ratio" — the most important retracement levelStrongest
0.786 (78.6%)Deep pullback — trend may be weakeningMedium

How to Draw Fibonacci Retracements

  • In an uptrend: Draw from swing LOW to swing HIGH. The retracement levels show where the pullback might find support.
  • In a downtrend: Draw from swing HIGH to swing LOW. Levels show where the bounce might find resistance.
  • Use significant swing points on your higher timeframe (Daily/4H), not every minor swing.

Extension Levels (Targets)

LevelUse
1.272Conservative take-profit target
1.618Standard extension target — very commonly hit
2.618Aggressive target — only in strong trends

Confluence is king: A Fibonacci level alone is interesting. A Fibonacci level that ALSO aligns with a horizontal S/R zone AND a moving average is a high-probability level. Always look for Fib levels that stack with other technical evidence.

The 0.618 "Golden Pocket"

The zone between the 0.618 and 0.65 retracement is called the "golden pocket." Professional traders consider this the highest-probability reversal zone in a trending market. If price pulls back to this zone and shows a candlestick reversal pattern with volume, it's one of the strongest trade setups in technical analysis.

Session Analysis

Markets behave differently depending on which global session is active. Understanding session characteristics helps you know WHEN to trade and when to sit out. This applies to both crypto (24/7 but volume varies) and forex.

The Three Major Sessions

SessionNY TimeUTC+4 ExampleCharacter
Asian (Tokyo)7 PM – 4 AM3 AM – 12 PMQuiet, range-bound. Sets the range that London often fakes out.
London3 AM – 12 PM11 AM – 8 PMHighest volume. Breakouts, trend initiation. Where the "Judas Swing" often occurs.
New York8 AM – 5 PM4 PM – 1 AMSecond highest volume. London-NY overlap (8-12 AM NY) is peak volatility.

Kill Zones (ICT Concept)

"Kill Zones" are specific windows within each session where the highest-probability moves occur:

  • London Kill Zone: 2–5 AM NY time (10 AM – 1 PM your time). This is where the Judas Swing typically forms.
  • New York Kill Zone: 8:30–11 AM NY time (4:30–7 PM your time). Major economic data releases happen here.
  • London Close Kill Zone: 10 AM – 12 PM NY time (6–8 PM your time). Reversal plays are common here.

Crypto-Specific Session Behavior

Crypto trades 24/7, but volume and volatility still follow session patterns because institutional crypto traders operate during business hours. BTC volume spikes during London and NY sessions. Asian session often sets a range that gets swept during London open.

The New York Midnight Open

The candle that opens at 00:00 New York time is the most important reference point in ICT methodology. It serves as the daily "equilibrium." Bullish days tend to trade above it. Bearish days tend to trade below. The Judas Swing is measured relative to this level.

Practical rule: Don't trade during Asian session unless you have a specific range-trading strategy. Your best setups will come during London and NY sessions when volume supports the move.

The 1% Rule

Position Sizing Formula

Risk Amount = Account × 0.01
Position Size = Risk Amount ÷ (Entry − Stop Loss)
Margin Required = (Position Size × Entry) ÷ Leverage

Multiple Timeframe Analysis

Professional traders never make decisions on a single timeframe. Top-down analysis means starting from the highest timeframe for direction and drilling down for entry precision. This is the difference between trading and guessing.

The Three-Screen Method

Developed by Dr. Alexander Elder (author of "Trading for a Living"), this approach uses three timeframes simultaneously:

ScreenTimeframePurpose
1: TrendDaily / WeeklyDetermine the DIRECTION. Are you bullish or bearish?
2: Trigger4HFind the SETUP. Is there a pattern at a key level?
3: Entry1H / 15mTime the ENTRY. Get the best price with a tight stop.

Top-Down Workflow

  • Step 1 (Daily): What's the macro trend? Price above 200 SMA = bullish bias. Market structure = HH/HL? You ONLY look for longs.
  • Step 2 (4H): Where is price relative to key S/R and Fibonacci levels? Is there a setup forming? Candlestick pattern at a zone?
  • Step 3 (1H/15m): Zoom in to find your exact entry. Look for a market structure shift, FVG, or order block entry on the lower timeframe.

The cardinal sin: Trading AGAINST the higher timeframe trend. If the Daily is bearish and you're trying to long on the 15-minute chart, you're fighting the current. The lower timeframe trade MUST align with the higher timeframe direction.

Timeframe Alignment Principle

The strongest trades occur when ALL three timeframes agree. Daily bullish + 4H at support + 1H showing reversal pattern = high-probability long. When timeframes conflict, the highest timeframe wins. Always.

Volume Profile & Order Flow

Basic volume bars show you HOW MUCH was traded. Volume Profile shows you WHERE it was traded. This is significantly more useful because it reveals the price levels where the most activity concentrated — the levels institutions care about.

Key Volume Profile Concepts

TermWhat It IsHow To Use It
Point of Control (POC)Price level with the highest traded volumeActs as a magnet — price tends to return to it. Strong S/R.
Value Area High (VAH)Upper boundary of 70% volume zoneResistance level. Price above = premium territory.
Value Area Low (VAL)Lower boundary of 70% volume zoneSupport level. Price below = discount territory.
High Volume NodesPrice areas with heavy tradingPrice moves slowly through these — consolidation zones.
Low Volume NodesPrice areas with minimal tradingPrice moves FAST through these — breakout zones.

Practical Application

  • Buy at VAL, sell at VAH: When price is ranging, the Value Area acts as the range boundaries.
  • POC as target: If price moves away from POC, it often returns. Use as a take-profit level.
  • Low Volume Node breakouts: When price enters an LVN, expect fast movement. These are the "air pockets" in the volume profile.

TradingView Setup

On TradingView, add "Volume Profile — Visible Range" (VPVR) from indicators. This overlays the volume profile on your chart showing exactly where the most trading occurred at each price level. It's free on the paid plan, but even the fixed range version is available on free tier.

Why this matters for leverage: Volume Profile tells you which levels are "defended" by heavy volume and which are "air." Placing your stop loss behind a High Volume Node means your stop is protected by a wall of orders. Placing it in a Low Volume Node means it could get run through easily.

Liquidity, Smart Money & The Judas Swing

Smart Money Concepts (SMC) is a framework developed primarily by Michael Huddleston (Inner Circle Trader / ICT) that explains HOW institutional traders manipulate price to fill their orders. This section covers the most practically useful concepts.

Liquidity — Why Stops Get Hunted

Institutions need liquidity to fill large orders. Retail stop losses are clusters of orders sitting at predictable levels (below support, above resistance). Smart money drives price INTO these stops to fill their own positions, then reverses.

  • Buy-side liquidity: Stop losses of short sellers sitting above resistance = pool of buy orders
  • Sell-side liquidity: Stop losses of long traders sitting below support = pool of sell orders
  • Liquidity sweep: Price briefly spikes through a key level, triggers stops, then reverses

Order Blocks

An order block is the last candle (or group of candles) before a strong impulsive move. It marks where institutional orders were placed. When price returns to this zone, it often finds support/resistance because unfilled institutional orders are still there.

  • Bullish OB: Last bearish candle before a strong up-move. When price returns, expect a bounce.
  • Bearish OB: Last bullish candle before a strong down-move. When price returns, expect rejection.

Fair Value Gaps (FVGs)

A Fair Value Gap is a three-candle pattern where the middle candle's body is so large that the wicks of candle 1 and candle 3 don't overlap, creating a "gap" in price. The market tends to return and fill these gaps before continuing.

FVGs are excellent entry zones — when price returns to fill the gap, you enter in the direction of the original move.

The Judas Swing

The Judas Swing is a false move at the start of a session designed to trap retail traders on the wrong side before the real move begins. Named after the biblical betrayal — the price "leads you to slaughter" then reverses.

  • Typically occurs between NY midnight and 5:00 AM NY time (8 AM – 1 PM your time)
  • Price breaks the Asian session range high or low, triggering breakout traders
  • Then reverses sharply, stopping out those traders and moving in the true daily direction
  • Look for it relative to the New York Midnight Opening Price — the daily equilibrium

How to Trade the Judas Swing

  • Determine daily bias from higher timeframe analysis first
  • Mark the Asian range high and low
  • Wait for the fake breakout during London open — price sweeps Asian high or low
  • Wait for Market Structure Shift (MSS) — a break of a swing point confirming reversal
  • Enter on the FVG or Order Block created by the reversal
  • Stop loss above/below the Judas Swing high/low
  • Target: opposite side of the Asian range or next liquidity pool

Warning: SMC/ICT concepts require significant screen time and practice to identify in real-time. Do NOT trade these live until you've backtested extensively on bar replay. The Judas Swing is an intermediate-to-advanced concept — master basic S/R, candlesticks, and risk management first.

The Wyckoff Method

Richard Wyckoff (1873–1934) studied how legendary operators like JP Morgan and Jesse Livermore moved markets. He codified their methods into a framework that explains how institutions build and unload massive positions. The Wyckoff Method has passed 100+ years of continuous use and remains the foundation of institutional price analysis.

The Composite Man

Wyckoff's central concept: imagine all institutional activity as ONE entity — the "Composite Man." This entity accumulates (buys) at lows, marks up (drives price higher), distributes (sells) at highs, then marks down (allows price to fall). Your job is to identify what the Composite Man is doing and trade WITH him, not against him.

The Four Market Phases

PhaseWhat HappensYour Action
AccumulationSideways range after downtrend. Institutions quietly buy. Volume decreases on drops, increases on rises.Prepare to go long. Watch for the "Spring."
MarkupBreakout above range. Strong uptrend with pullbacks to support. Higher highs and higher lows.Hold long positions. Add on pullbacks to S/R.
DistributionSideways range after uptrend. Institutions sell to retail. Volume increases on drops.Prepare to exit longs. Watch for the "Upthrust."
MarkdownBreakdown below range. Strong downtrend. Lower highs and lower lows.Short or stay flat. Don't try to catch the falling knife.

Key Wyckoff Events

  • Spring: Price dips BELOW accumulation range support, shakes out weak holders, then reverses back inside. This is the buy signal. (Similar to a liquidity sweep in SMC.)
  • Upthrust (UT): Price spikes ABOVE distribution range resistance, traps late buyers, then reverses back inside. This is the sell signal.
  • Sign of Strength (SOS): A strong move up within accumulation range — institutions are ready.
  • Last Point of Support (LPS): Final pullback before the markup begins. Ideal entry.

Wyckoff in Crypto

Crypto markets exhibit textbook Wyckoff patterns because they're heavily influenced by whale activity (the Composite Man equivalent). Bitcoin's 2015-2016 accumulation before the 2017 bull run, and the 2022 accumulation before the 2023-2024 rally, both followed classic Wyckoff schematics.

Practical application: When BTC is in a long sideways range after a significant downtrend, apply Wyckoff. Is volume decreasing on red candles? Is the price holding support? Is there a Spring event? If yes — accumulation is likely in progress. The markup phase will follow.

Phase 3 · Week 10 Supplement

Advanced Trade Management

The v1 playbook said "hit TP or hit SL." That's beginner-level trade management. Real trading involves managing open positions actively to maximize winners and minimize losers. These techniques come from professional trading desks and prop firms.

Moving Stop to Breakeven

Once your trade moves 1R in profit (risk amount), move your stop loss to your entry price. You're now in a risk-free trade. The worst outcome is $0 instead of -1R.

Don't move to breakeven too early. Give the trade room to breathe. If you move to BE when the trade is only +0.3R, normal volatility will stop you out at zero on trades that would have been winners.

Partial Take Profits

Instead of all-or-nothing (full TP or full SL), take profits in stages:

  • At 1R profit: Close 33% of position, move stop to breakeven on the rest
  • At 2R profit: Close another 33%, trail stop to +1R
  • Let the final 33% run with a trailing stop — this is where outsized winners come from

Trailing Stop Methods

MethodHow It WorksBest For
Structure-basedMove stop below each new higher low (longs) or above each new lower high (shorts)Swing trades — respects market structure
EMA trailTrail stop below the 20 EMA — exit when price closes below itTrending markets — stays in trade longer
ATR trailStop = current price minus 1.5-2x ATRVolatile markets — adapts to conditions

Scaling In (Pyramiding)

Adding to a winning position — but ONLY if the original position is already in profit and the setup still valid. Never scale into a loser.

  • First position at your initial entry with full risk
  • Second position on a confirmed pullback to support — half the size of the first
  • Total risk on the combined position must still not exceed 2% of account

The #1 rule: NEVER add to a losing position with leverage. "Averaging down" with leverage is the fastest way to liquidation. Your stop was hit for a reason. Accept the loss.

No asset trades in isolation. Understanding what moves together (positive correlation) and what moves opposite (negative correlation) gives you an informational edge and prevents you from taking conflicting positions.

SMT Divergence (Smart Money Technique)

An ICT concept: when two correlated assets (like BTC and ETH) make new highs/lows but one DOESN'T confirm the other, it signals a potential reversal. Example:

  • BTC makes a new low, but ETH makes a higher low → bullish SMT divergence
  • BTC makes a new high, but ETH makes a lower high → bearish SMT divergence

This works because if smart money is buying, they'd buy BOTH. If only one is making new extremes, it's likely a fake move.

Practical Correlation Workflow

  • Check DXY direction before ANY crypto trade
  • Check BTC before trading ETH — if BTC is weak, ETH will be weaker
  • Check S&P 500 futures during overlap hours for risk sentiment
  • Don't hold long BTC AND long ETH simultaneously — that's doubled risk, not diversification

Backtesting Methodology

Backtesting is how you validate your strategy before risking money. But bad backtesting gives you false confidence, which is worse than no backtesting at all. Here's how to do it properly.

The Right Way to Backtest

  • Use bar replay on TradingView. Scroll the chart left so you can't see the future. Advance candle by candle. Make decisions in real-time, not hindsight.
  • Sample size: minimum 50 trades. Below 50, your statistics are meaningless. Random variance dominates.
  • Test across different market conditions: trending up, trending down, ranging. A strategy that only works in uptrends is useless half the time.
  • Record EVERYTHING in your journal — same template as live trades.

Metrics to Track

MetricFormulaTarget
Win RateWinning trades ÷ total trades≥ 40% (at 1:2 R:R)
Average R:RAverage win ÷ average loss≥ 1.5:1
Expectancy(Win% × Avg Win) − (Loss% × Avg Loss)Positive number
Max DrawdownLargest peak-to-trough decline< 15% of account
Profit FactorGross profits ÷ gross losses> 1.5
Max Consecutive LossesLongest losing streakKnow this number. It WILL happen live.

Common Backtesting Mistakes

  • Hindsight bias: "I would have taken that trade" — if you can see the outcome, your brain cheats. Use bar replay ONLY.
  • Cherry-picking: Only testing on periods where your strategy works. Test on BAD periods too.
  • Curve-fitting: Tweaking rules until they fit historical data perfectly. Overfitted strategies fail on new data.
  • Ignoring slippage and fees: On leveraged trades, funding rates and fees eat into profits. Account for them.

The backtest litmus test: If your backtested win rate is above 70%, something is wrong. Either you're using hindsight, cherry-picking, or curve-fitting. Real-world strategies typically have 40-60% win rates with good R:R. Suspiciously good results are a red flag, not a green one.
